What Is a Near Rhyme? How Slant Rhymes Make Your Lyrics Better

Published: May 2026  ·  Updated: June 2026  ·  By Athan Natsikas

Every songwriter hits the same wall eventually. You have the perfect line. The emotion is right, the rhythm is right, the image is exactly what you wanted. And then you need a rhyme, and nothing fits.

The word you need does not have a perfect rhyme. Or the perfect rhyme exists, but it is a cliche you have heard in a thousand songs before. So you force something in, the line dies, and the whole verse loses its energy.

Near rhymes fix this problem. And once you understand how to use them, you will never feel stuck on a rhyme again.

What Is a Near Rhyme?

A near rhyme (also called a slant rhyme, half rhyme, or imperfect rhyme) is when two words share similar but not identical sounds. They do not match perfectly, but they are close enough that your ear registers the connection.

"Heart" and "hurt" are a near rhyme. "Fire" and "far" are a near rhyme. "Soul" and "still" are a near rhyme.

None of these are perfect matches, but in a song they work because the human ear is remarkably good at finding patterns, even approximate ones. Near rhymes are everywhere in popular music. You just have not been listening for them by name.

Why Near Rhymes Are More Useful Than Perfect Rhymes

Perfect rhymes feel satisfying. "Love" and "above," "time" and "rhyme," "night" and "light." They land with a click that listeners find pleasing.

But perfect rhymes have a serious limitation. There are not enough of them. For every word you want to rhyme, there are only a handful of perfect matches, and most of them have already been used in songs you grew up listening to. The moment you write fire and reach for "desire," you are borrowing from a long line of songwriters who got there first.

Near rhymes expand your vocabulary by a factor of ten. Instead of five perfect rhymes for a word, you suddenly have fifty near rhymes to choose from. That means you can prioritize the word that fits the meaning, the rhythm, and the emotion of your line, rather than settling for whatever happens to rhyme perfectly.

The best songwriters have always known this. They use near rhymes not as a compromise, but as a deliberate choice.

Near Rhymes in Songs You Already Know

Slant rhymes show up constantly in music across every genre. Once you start noticing them, you cannot stop.

Thom Yorke of Radiohead built entire albums on near rhymes. The slight mismatch between sounds creates a feeling of unease and tension that perfectly serves the music's emotional content. The rhyme does not quite resolve, and neither does the feeling.

Country music leans on near rhymes to keep lyrics sounding natural and conversational. A perfect rhyme can feel forced in a storytelling song. A near rhyme lets the words sit in the mouth the way real speech does.

In hip-hop, near rhymes give rappers room to say exactly what they mean. Kendrick Lamar, Jay-Z, and countless others use slant rhyme to maintain flow without sacrificing specificity. The rhyme is there. It is just wearing different clothes.

Even the word pages on RhymeIt prove this. Look at the results for words like soul or truth. The near rhyme section is always longer, richer, and more surprising than the perfect rhyme section. That is not an accident.

The Difference Between Near Rhymes and Bad Rhymes

Not every imperfect rhyme is a near rhyme. There is a difference between a deliberate slant rhyme and a rhyme that just does not work.

A near rhyme shares a significant sound with its partner. It might share the vowel sound ("heart" and "dark"), the consonant ending ("soul" and "still"), or the overall sonic shape of the word. The connection is real, even if it is not perfect.

A bad rhyme is when two words are forced together because the writer ran out of options. There is no genuine sonic connection, just desperation. Listeners feel this immediately, even if they cannot name what is wrong.

The test is simple. Say the two words out loud, back to back. Does your ear find a connection? Does it feel intentional? If yes, it is a near rhyme. If it sounds like you gave up, it is a bad rhyme.

How to Use Near Rhymes in Your Own Writing

The practical shift is smaller than you think.

When you get stuck on a rhyme, instead of searching only for perfect matches, expand your search to include words that share part of the sound. If you are writing a line that ends with dream, do not just look for words that rhyme perfectly. Look for words that share the long "ee" vowel sound, like "breathe," "beneath," or "believe." Look for words that end in a similar consonant cluster, like "gleam," "stream," or "seem." Then look further out, at words that carry a similar sonic weight even if the match is loose.

Near Rhymes by Syllable Count — Why It Matters

One thing most rhyme finders miss is that a near rhyme has to fit your line rhythmically, not just sonically. A three-syllable near rhyme will not work in a line built around one-syllable punches, no matter how good the sound match is.

This is why RhymeIt organizes every near rhyme result by syllable count. When you search for near rhymes for heart, night, or light, you can filter instantly by how many syllables you need. One syllable for a punchy rap bar. Two syllables for a flowing pop chorus. Three or more for a complex verse structure.

The syllable count is often what separates a near rhyme that works from one that almost works.

One Rule Worth Keeping

Near rhymes give you freedom, but freedom without intention produces noise. The best slant rhymes feel chosen, not accidental. When you use a near rhyme, make sure the word you have chosen is the best possible word for that line, not just a word that sort of sounds like something else.

The rhyme is in service of the song. Always.

If the near rhyme is the best word for the line, use it without apology. If you are using it because you could not find anything better, keep searching. The difference is intention, and listeners can feel it even when they cannot explain it.

Near rhymes are not a shortcut or a compromise. They are a tool, and in the hands of a skilled songwriter, they are often more powerful than perfect rhymes because they give you the word you actually need.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a near rhyme?

A near rhyme (also called a slant rhyme, half rhyme, or imperfect rhyme) is when two words share similar but not identical sounds. They do not match perfectly, but they are close enough that your ear registers the connection.

What is the difference between a near rhyme and a perfect rhyme?

A perfect rhyme shares identical vowel and consonant sounds from the stressed syllable onward. A near rhyme shares only part of that sound, creating an approximate match that the ear still registers as connected. Near rhymes give you far more word choices than perfect rhymes.

Do professional songwriters use near rhymes?

Yes. Kendrick Lamar, Jay-Z, Thom Yorke, and countless others use slant rhymes deliberately to maintain flow without sacrificing the word they actually need. Near rhymes are a tool, not a compromise.
